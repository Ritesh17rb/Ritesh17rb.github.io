![Post-gym selfie, gloves still on](/blog/assets/post-gym-selfie.webp)

That's me, right after a gym session. Sweaty, tired, gloves still on — and honestly, happier than I've been in a long time.

I joined the gym on **18th May**. No grand plan, no transformation challenge, no "new me" post. I just walked in one evening because I was tired of feeling tired. This is what one month of resistance training — plus **15-20 km of running every week** — has actually done to me.

## The first week was humbling

Let me be honest: I thought I was reasonably fit. I was not.

The first few sessions, I was lifting weights that felt embarrassingly light, and I was still sore for two days after. Walking down stairs after leg day is a special kind of comedy. But something about that soreness felt *earned*. It was the first time in a while that my body was tired for a good reason, not just from sitting at a desk all day.

## The routine that actually stuck

I've tried routines before. They died in a week. This one stuck, and I think it's because I kept it stupidly simple:

- **Gym, 5-6 days a week.** Push, pull, legs — repeat. Nothing fancy. Progressive overload: if I lifted it easily last week, I add a little more this week.
- **Running, 15-20 km spread across the week.** Some days it's a slow 3-4 km just to move. One longer run when I'm feeling good. I don't chase pace, I chase consistency.
- **No zero days.** Even on the worst day, I do *something* — a short run, a light session, a walk. The streak matters more than any single workout.

The biggest change wasn't in the plan. It was in how I treat it: the workout is an **appointment, not an option**. I don't negotiate with myself at 6 PM anymore. I just go.

## What it did to my work

This is the part nobody told me about.

I expected to be *more* tired after adding workouts to my day. The opposite happened. My focus at work is noticeably sharper — I sit down and actually work, instead of drifting between tabs. The afternoon slump that used to eat my day? Mostly gone.

I think it's because the gym taught me something that transferred directly to work: **you do the rep even when you don't feel like it, and the feeling follows the action, not the other way around.** Opening a hard task now feels like unracking a heavy bar. You don't wait for motivation. You just start, and ten minutes in, you're in it.

Also, having a fixed workout slot forced structure onto everything else. When your evening has a non-negotiable block, you stop wasting the hours around it.

## The emotional part (the real reason I'm writing this)

I'll say this plainly: the mental change is bigger than the physical one.

Running did something for my head that nothing else has. There's a point around the 3rd or 4th kilometre where the noise just... stops. Whatever I was overthinking — work, people, the future — it gets quieter with every step. Some of my clearest thinking this month happened on the road, drenched in sweat, music in my ears.

![Right after a 5 km run](/blog/assets/after-5k-run-selfie.webp)

That's what the end of a 5 km run looks like. Completely drained, completely clear-headed.

And the gym gave me proof. That's the word — **proof**. Every week there's evidence that I'm capable of more than last week. A little more weight. One more rep. A run that felt impossible in May and feels normal now. You can't argue with a logbook.

That proof leaks into everything. I stand differently. I speak up a bit more easily. When something difficult comes up, my default reaction has shifted from *"can I handle this?"* to *"okay, how do I handle this?"* That's what people call confidence, I think. It's not loud. It's just the quiet memory of hard things you've already done.

## What actually changed in one month

- I sleep like a rock. In bed earlier, asleep faster, awake before the alarm.
- I eat more consciously — not a strict diet, but I finally think about protein, and junk food lost its grip because I don't want to waste a workout.
- My energy is stable through the day instead of spiking and crashing.
- Clothes fit differently. Small thing, doesn't matter, absolutely matters.
- I look forward to the hard part of the day. That's new.

## If you're thinking about starting

You don't need the perfect plan, the perfect gym, or the perfect time in your life. I started on a random Monday in May with no idea what I was doing. One month in, I'm not a different person — I'm the same person with proof that I show up. That turns out to be enough to change almost everything else.

One month down. This is just the start.
